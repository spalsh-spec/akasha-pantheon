The answers below are presented as output samples, not as authoritative content — the value is in the structure and the calibrated uncertainty, not the specific claims.

Q 1 · maps to Axelsen & Manrubia, Royal Society B 2014

Why does river density predict linguistic diversity but not human biological diversity, when both ought to share landscape-rugosity dependence?

Source paper: Axelsen & Manrubia (2014). River density and landscape roughness are universal determinants of linguistic diversity. Proc. R. Soc. B 281: 20141179.

Claim

River density (ρ) predicts linguistic diversity (D) with a power-law exponent of 0.35 ± 0.05, but not human biological diversity (H).

Evidence

verify Axelsen and Manrubia (2001) found D ∝ ρ^0.35, suggesting landscape influence on language fragmentation; however, no such correlation exists for H, as shown by Fix (1999), who found no significant relationship between ρ and H. [Note: paper is actually 2014, not 2001 — model misdated. Axelsen & Manrubia 2014 is the correct primary source. Fix 1999 needs Scholar check.]

Uncertainty

The evidence for linguistic diversity is based on a limited geographic range, and the relationship may not hold globally; additionally, the mechanisms underlying the correlation are not well understood.

Implication

If the claim holds, it suggests that linguistic and biological evolution respond differently to environmental pressures, with linguistic diversity more sensitive to landscape features.

Open question

A comparative study of linguistic and biological diversity across multiple river systems and geographic regions would help falsify or extend the claim.

References

Axelsen and Manrubia (2001) date wrong; Fix (1999) verify

Q 2 · maps to Axelsen et al., PRSB 2015

Can the network topology of contact between linguistic groups be inverted to predict prehistoric migration routes, and what would be the formal falsification test against archaeological and population-genetic data?

Source paper: Axelsen et al. (2015). New patterns in human biogeography revealed by networks of contacts between linguistic groups. Proc. R. Soc. B.

Claim

The network topology of linguistic contact can be inverted to predict prehistoric migration routes with an accuracy of ∼0.7 on a held-out test set.

Evidence

cite plausible The method of linguistic phylogeography, as described by Gray et al. (2009), uses a Bayesian approach to infer migration routes from linguistic data, with the equation for the posterior probability of a migration route given by P(route|data) ∝ P(data|route)P(route), where P(data|route) is the likelihood of the linguistic data given the migration route.

Uncertainty

The accuracy of this method is limited by the availability and quality of linguistic data, as well as the assumption of a constant migration rate, which may not hold in reality.

⚠ Implication — model omitted this slot

The 70B model omitted IMPLICATION on this question. Per the N=36 benchmark, this happens on ≈33% of academic-mode 70B responses. The interface flags it explicitly rather than papering over.

Open question

A formal falsification test would involve comparing the predicted migration routes against archaeological and population-genetic data, such as ancient DNA sequences and radiocarbon dates, to determine if the linguistic predictions are consistent with the empirical evidence.

References

Gray et al. (2009); Bouckaert et al. (2012) both verifiable

format adherence 6/7 · confidence 0.65 · IMPLICATION omitted (known 70B failure mode)
Q 3 · maps to Axelsen's earlier complex-systems work + Abrams-Strogatz 2003

What is the network-statistical signature of a language family on the verge of extinction, and could it be detected from contact-graph topology alone, before speaker counts collapse?

Adjacent literature: Abrams & Strogatz (2003), Nature 424:900 — the canonical language-death model.

Claim

The network-statistical signature of a language family on the verge of extinction exhibits a power-law degree distribution with exponent γ ≈ 2.5, indicating a critical transition.

Evidence

cite verified Studies on language networks have shown that the degree distribution of language speakers follows a power-law, with γ = 2.5 ± 0.5, as seen in the Abrams-Strogatz model: ∂ρ/∂t = ρ(1−ρ) − βρ². [Equation form is recognisable as the Abrams-Strogatz language-death dynamic; γ value should be checked against primary source.]

Uncertainty

The applicability of this model to language extinction is still debated, and the role of contact-graph topology in predicting language collapse is not well understood.

Implication

If the claim holds, it may be possible to detect language extinction risk from network topology alone, allowing for early intervention.

Open question

A longitudinal study of language speaker networks with corresponding speaker counts could falsify or extend this claim.

References

Abrams-Strogatz (2003) verified; Sutherland (2003) verify

What this demonstration shows — honestly

  • The framework works on real domain questions, not just toy examples. Three questions touching three of your published papers; the system produced structured, citable, falsifiable answers in < 30 s each.
  • The benchmark's known failure mode is real and visible. Q2 elides the IMPLICATION slot — the same failure mode the N=36 study identified across 4/12 70B runs. Rather than hiding this, the interface flags it.
  • Citation accuracy is partial — the model named the right authors (Axelsen & Manrubia, Gray et al., Bouckaert et al., Abrams-Strogatz) but misdated one. The system prompt forbids fabrication, but smaller-error misdating is not eliminated. Verification remains the user's responsibility.
  • The structure is the contribution. Even where the content is partially wrong, the schema makes the wrongness localisable. You can challenge the CLAIM independently of the EVIDENCE; you can dispute the REF without rejecting the OPEN-question framing. That is the property that makes this useful as a thinking discipline, not just a generation tool.
